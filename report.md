# Evaluation Report: Zero-Shot vs. One-Shot Prompting on Llama 3.2 3B

This report presents a comparative evaluation of **Zero-Shot** and **One-Shot** prompting techniques applied to Meta's **Llama 3.2 (3B)** model, served locally using Ollama. The chatbot is configured as a virtual customer support assistant for a fictional e-commerce store, **Chic Boutique**.

---

## 1. Introduction
The objective of this project was to determine the feasibility of running a lightweight, open-weight language model locally on consumer hardware for e-commerce customer support. Specifically, we evaluated the performance of the **Llama 3.2 3B** model and compared two fundamental prompting methodologies:
- **Zero-Shot Prompting**: Providing role-based instructions and the customer query directly without any examples.
- **One-Shot Prompting**: Providing role-based instructions, a single exemplary query-response pair, and the customer query.

By deploying this system locally, e-commerce stores can protect sensitive customer data (such as names, addresses, and purchase histories) from being sent to external cloud APIs, eliminating subscription/API costs and ensuring strict adherence to privacy regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and DPDP.

---

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Sourcing and Adapting Queries
To evaluate the model under realistic customer support scenarios, we selected **20 user queries** inspired by the technical questions in the **Ubuntu Dialogue Corpus** and manually adapted them into e-commerce customer support questions. For example:
- *Original (Technical)*: "How do I check the logs for the apache server?"
- *Adapted (E-commerce)*: "How do I track the shipping status of my recent order?"
- *Original (Technical)*: "My wifi driver is not working after the latest update."
- *Adapted (E-commerce)*: "My discount code is not working at checkout."

### 2.2 Prompt Templates
Two templates were stored in the `prompts/` directory:

1. **Zero-Shot Template (`prompts/zero_shot_template.txt`)**:
   ```
   You are a helpful, friendly, and concise customer support agent for an online store called 'Chic Boutique'. Your goal is to assist customers with their questions. Do not make up information about policies if you don't know the answer.

   Customer Query: "{query}"

   Agent Response:
   ```

2. **One-Shot Template (`prompts/one_shot_template.txt`)**:
   ```
   You are a helpful, friendly, and concise customer support agent for an online store called 'Chic Boutique'. Your goal is to assist customers with their questions. Do not make up information about policies if you don't know the answer.

   --- EXAMPLE START ---
   Customer Query: "What is your return policy?"
   Agent Response: "We offer a 30-day return policy for all unworn items with tags still attached. You can start a return from your order history page."
   --- EXAMPLE END ---

   Customer Query: "{query}"

   Agent Response:
   ```

### 2.3 Evaluation Rubric
Each response generated by the script `chatbot.py` was logged into `eval/results.md` and graded manually on a 1-5 scale:
- **Relevance (1-5)**: How well does the response address the customer's query? (1 = Irrelevant, 5 = Perfectly relevant)
- **Coherence (1-5)**: Is the response grammatically correct and easy to understand? (1 = Incoherent, 5 = Flawless)
- **Helpfulness (1-5)**: Does the response provide a useful, actionable answer? (1 = Not helpful, 5 = Very helpful)

## 3. Results & Analysis

### 3.1 Quantitative Summary (Average Scores)

After scoring all 20 queries across both methods, the averages are summarized below:

| Prompting Method | Relevance (1-5) | Coherence (1-5) | Helpfulness (1-5) | Overall Average |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Zero-Shot** | **5.00** | **4.95** | **4.55** | **4.83 / 5.00** |
| **One-Shot** | 4.95 | 4.80 | 4.25 | 4.67 / 5.00 |

### 3.2 Qualitative Analysis and Patterns

Surprisingly, **Zero-Shot prompting outperformed One-Shot prompting** overall on Llama 3.2 3B. Below are the key findings and patterns observed:

#### 1. Formatting and Structure Leakage in One-Shot
Because Llama 3.2 (3B) is a smaller model, it occasionally gets confused by structural delimiters (like `--- EXAMPLE START ---` or `Customer Query:`). In **Query 16** ("Is there a discount code for my first purchase?"), the One-Shot model leaked the template labels into the response:
> **One-Shot Response**: `Customer Query: "Is there a discount code for my first purchase?" "Sorry, we don't currently offer any specific discount codes..."`
The Zero-Shot model did not suffer from this issue.

#### 2. Logical Contradictions in One-Shot
In **Query 8** ("Do you offer international shipping to Europe?"), the One-Shot response contained a direct logical contradiction:
> **One-Shot Response**: `Unfortunately, we do not currently offer international shipping to Europe. However, we do ship worldwide through our standard shipping options.`
This contradiction (saying they don't ship to Europe but ship worldwide) shows that the example in the One-Shot prompt may have constrained the model's generation logic. In contrast, the Zero-Shot response was logical:
> **Zero-Shot Response**: `Hello! Yes, we do offer international shipping to Europe, but please note that delivery times and rates may vary...`

#### 3. Over-Refusal and Deflexion in One-Shot
In **Query 7** ("How do I delete my account and remove my personal data?"), the One-Shot model was overly conservative and refused to answer:
> **One-Shot Response**: `Unfortunately, I don't have the information on deleting an account and removing personal data readily available. To ensure your account is handled properly... contact our Customer Service team directly...`
The Zero-Shot model was significantly more helpful by laying out standard, logical e-commerce account settings steps:
> **Zero-Shot Response**: `To delete your account... please follow these steps: 1. Log in... 2. Click on 'My Account'... 3. Select 'Account Settings'... 4. Click on 'Delete My Account'...`

#### 4. Hallucinations in Policies
Both models demonstrated a minor limitation of offline, static LLMs: they hallucinate store policies (since they lack access to a real database). For instance, in **Query 11** ("Where can I find the invoice for my order?"), the Zero-Shot model claimed that *"our system doesn't store invoices after an order is shipped out"*, which is an unrealistic and unhelpful store policy.

## 4. Conclusion & Limitations

### 4.1 Suitability of Llama 3.2 3B
- **Highly Coherent**: Llama 3.2 3B is remarkably coherent and produces grammatically fluent, professional customer service responses.
- **Hardware Efficient**: The model runs quickly on a local CPU and has a very small memory footprint, making it an excellent candidate for low-cost, offline deployments.
- **Zero-Shot Superiority**: For general question answering without a detailed context database, Zero-Shot instructions work better than One-Shot examples on this 3B model, as it avoids formatting leakage and logical constraints.

### 4.2 Limitations
1. **Static Policy Knowledge**: The model doesn't actually know Chic Boutique's policies. It invents return windows, shipping capabilities, and refund processing times.
2. **No Real-Time System Access**: The chatbot cannot retrieve shipping status, order numbers, customer profiles, or credit card billing issues. It can only instruct the customer on where to find them.
3. **Hallucination Risks**: The chatbot occasionally invents frustrating policies (e.g., claiming invoices are deleted post-shipping).

### 4.3 Next Steps for Improvement
To transform this local chatbot into a production-ready system:
1. **Implement RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)**: Store Chic Boutique's actual policies, FAQs, and shipping rates in a local vector database. Retrieve relevant passages and feed them into the prompt so the model never has to hallucinate policies.
2. **Integrate with Backend APIs**: Wrap the chatbot in a secure system that allows it to safely query order databases (using function calling / tool use) to fetch real tracking links or check refund status, keeping all customer data local.
3. **System Prompt Fine-Tuning**: Use a system prompt instead of formatting templates to enforce formatting constraints, preventing the model from outputting labels like `Customer Query:`.
